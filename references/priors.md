## FLOORING / TRADES

**Adjacency triggers:** flooring, carpet, LVT, laminate, vinyl, resin, epoxy,
screed, subfloor, floor fitting/fitter, floor installer, underlay.

### Expertise level (where the real edge is)
- **Hands-on, expert:** carpet (strongest), LVT, laminate, sheet vinyl.
- **Network/contacts (not hands-on):** resin, epoxy, screeding — can speak to it
  via trusted contacts, but flag resin pricing/process as second-hand.
- **No experience:** tiling.
> Content edge is strongest on carpet/LVT/laminate. Resin/epoxy/screed content
> should lean on contacts and be marked as such, not faked as first-hand.

### The competitor "wall" (who actually shows up)
- **Local retailers (Horsham), multi-flooring, have showrooms:** Supafit,
  Berwicks of Horsham, James for Carpets. (Zack has worked for/with these.)
- **Independents:** no showroom, source materials from retailers like Supafit,
  fit their own jobs. Get work largely by **word-of-mouth** — no other lead source.
- **Directory:** Checkatrade recurs; some MyBuilder/Rated People.
- **How customers find installers:** Google "near me" dominates; some Checkatrade;
  word-of-mouth huge for independents.
- **Facebook posts/groups:** good for installers/workers chasing private jobs —
  posts often get comments recommending people. Unknown how many actual customer
  enquiries they generate (Zack has only seen this as a worker, never as a
  customer), so treat as an installer-side channel, not a proven demand source.
> SOFT SERP space (small independents + one directory), NOT an affiliate/brand
> wall like website-builder niches. Winnable for an indie.

### Monetisation reality
- **Primary candidate: lead-gen — but ENTIRELY UNTESTED.** Zack has never sold a
  lead to anyone, any size firm. Plausible, not proven.
- **Lead value scales with job size, not firm size:**
  - High-value jobs (full-house, resin, big LVT, insurance-payout work) → worth a
    bigger firm's time → most sellable, best-paying leads.
  - Small/cheap jobs (single room, budget carpet, price-shoppers) → suit
    independents who need any work; low resale value.
  - **Caveat — big firms DO take small jobs in two conditions:** (a) when work is
    plentiful, to capture all customers in a busy market, or (b) when work is
    tight, taking anything to cover bills. So "firm size → job size" is a tendency,
    not a rule — demand conditions shift who'll take what.
- Customers typically chase the **cheapest** option → price-shopper leads are
  weak; qualify leads by job value before treating them as a sellable asset.
- **monetisation_tag:** lead-gen-local (primary), with ads-display /
  affiliate-physical as content-site fallbacks. NOT affiliate-saas.

### Buyer types (who the content speaks to)
- **Everyday replacement** (the obvious majority) — people who just want a change,
  or whose carpet/flooring is worn, damaged, or dated. The baseline demand.
- **Elderly** increasing property value to pass on (inheritance).
- **New-home movers** updating/improving a property.
- **Sellers** boosting value before sale.
- **Flood / insurance-payout** customers using a payout for new flooring — a sharp
  sub-niche of its own.

### Insider knowledge bank (first-hand specificity for RANK content)
Real detail a non-trade writer cannot produce. (Pricing referenced to a ~4×4 room.)

**Pricing reality (UK, fitting):**
- Carpet: ~£5.50/m² full prep room; ~£3.50/m² fit-only (prep already done).
  Simple fitting often ~£100/room.
  - **Door trimming extra:** a risen floor level (thicker underlay and/or carpet)
    can stop doors opening, so fitters cut/trim the doors — seen charged ~£15–25
    each. Mainly a carpet issue, since carpet raises the floor level the most.
- LVT straight-lay: ~£15–30/m² fit-only (roughly double when supplying material,
  product-dependent). Herringbone / basket-weave: significantly more.
- Sheet vinyl: ~£100–150 fitting; may need hardboard/ply subfloor first.
- Resin: more expensive than LVT (second-hand from contact; Zack hasn't fitted).

**How fitters actually charge (varies — there's no single model):**
- A fitter may work a **day rate** (and make margin by marking up materials), a
  **per-m²** rate, or a **mix** depending on the job. Honestly down to the fitter.
- **Furniture moving** is often charged separately (it's time away from fitting) —
  Zack's seen ~£20–40 per unit depending on the firm.
- **Screeding example (Zack's contact):** ~£6.50–9.50/m² for jobs under a day; a
  full-day screed switches to a **day rate**; beyond a day he may switch back to
  per-m². Shows how trade pricing flexes by job length, not a fixed sheet.

**Materials reality:**
- Good-quality bag of screed: ~£40 (coverage per bag: TBC — confirm the m²).
- **Always over-order materials "just in case"** — a sensible buffer over the
  measured amount to cover mistakes, offcuts, or issues mid-job. Not a ridiculous
  amount, but enough that you don't run short and stall the job.
- **Price varies by quality** across every material — prep materials (screed,
  compound, underlay, ply) and finish materials (carpet, LVT, vinyl, resin) all
  span budget to premium. So any quoted figure is a band, not a fixed price; the
  spec/quality chosen moves the cost a lot.

**What goes wrong (failure modes):**
- **Skipping uplift of old laminate** before carpeting → big height mismatch (must
  account for carpet + underlay height).
- **Mis-measured rooms** — or rooms measured then later extended/altered, so the
  order no longer fits.
- **Carpet coming up short on width.** Carpets are sold as 4m or 5m widths but
  actually arrive a few mm under (typically 1–3mm short). If a measurement is
  tight to the nominal width, the carpet can land short across the room. (Length
  isn't the issue — that's whatever's rolled off the manufacturer's roll — it's
  the fixed width that catches you out.)
- **Laying on under-cured screed** → weight of flooring can crush the not-yet-set
  screed below.
- **Screed in 25°C+ heat** dries too fast → cracking. Non-structural cracks fixed
  with a feather/compound (thicker screed for small holes/cracks) so there are no
  voids under the new floor.
- **Product/manufacturing faults in the carpet itself:**
  - **Mis-dye** — delivered carpet looks different to the in-store sample or the
    online photo (colour off).
  - **Line faults** — the most prevalent manufacturing defect, though still not
    frequent.
  - **Oil stains / rips** — uncommon, but they do happen.
  These arrive from the manufacturer, so they're not a fitting error — but they
  derail a job and need spotting before laying.

**Prep nobody mentions:**
- Hoover + ensure subfloor is **dry and dust-free** (critical for hard floors so
  glue bonds and no mould forms; still matters for carpet).
- **Level** the subfloor — most screed needs a light sand to remove lumps/bumps;
  raises show through resin, LVT, vinyl (and big lumps even through carpet).

**Timing truths:**
- Affected by: furniture in the property, number of rooms, and whether it's full
  prep / half prep / fit-only.
- Usually done **after decorating**, but jobs can mark walls/skirtings.
